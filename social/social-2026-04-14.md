If you moved your parent into care this week and you are sitting at your own kitchen table right now with a cold cup of coffee and that voice in your head saying *this is not working, I should bring her home*, please read this before you do anything.

What you are seeing is not evidence that you made the wrong choice. What you are seeing is what the first weeks look like. Almost nobody tells families this ahead of time.

## 4. LINKEDIN POST (healthcare professionals + discharge planners + referral network)

**Suggested image concept:** Warm, professional shot of a quiet residential room with natural light, a cardigan on a chair, framed photo on a nightstand, soft sage blanket. Implies a landed, lived-in space, not a facility.

**Post copy:**

A quiet observation for my colleagues in discharge planning, social work, and primary care across King County.

When a family moves a parent into residential care, the first two to four weeks are where placements are won or lost. Not on the tour. Not on the paperwork. On day four, when the parent seems more confused, eats less, and asks to go home, and the adult child sits at her own kitchen table and writes the word *mistake?* on a legal pad.

Published research and clinical practice both describe this as the transition dip. A temporary, normal adjustment period characterized by disorientation, flattened appetite, disrupted sleep, and relocation stress. It is almost always a dip, not a cliff. The overall adjustment period is typically three to six months, and the hardest stretch is the first two to four weeks.

What breaks placements in that window is not the dip itself. It is the silence around it. Families who were never told that the transition gets harder before it gets easier interpret the dip as evidence the placement is failing and pull their parent home, often into a second crisis.

Two implications for those of us in the referral pathway.

First, the normalization conversation belongs at discharge, not at month two. A short, clear, repeated message that week one to four may look like worsening and is almost always landing saves families from mid transition reversals.

Second, the small home model compresses the dip. In a six bed adult family home where the same two or three caregivers see the resident every day, preference learning takes days, not months. The behavioral smoothing is proportionally faster.

At Burien Best Care Home, we walk our Burien and King County families through the first ninety days with that timeline named out loud. If you have a family in week one or week two right now, please remind them that what they are seeing is the process, not the mistake.

Happy to coordinate on any family navigating a transition in our area.
