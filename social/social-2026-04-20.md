**Post copy:**

Your phone rings at 3:12 on a Tuesday afternoon.

It is the social worker at the skilled nursing facility where your mother has been since the hospital discharged her seventeen days ago. She is polite. She is fast. She says there is a form she needs you to come in and sign today. A pink triplicate. The letters across the top say Notice of Medicare Non-Coverage. The cutoff is Thursday at 11:59 PM.

You ask what this means.

She says, in the careful way people say it when they have said it eight hundred times, "Your mom has plateaued. Medicare will no longer pay for her stay."

You thought you had until July. You thought you had a hundred days. Yesterday, she walked twenty feet with a walker. The day before, she remembered the name of your grandson's dog. You are standing in the Burien Fred Meyer parking lot with a bag of rotisserie chicken in the passenger seat, and suddenly you have forty-eight hours to figure out where your mother is going to live the rest of her life.

I am writing this for the woman in that car.

Three things, before you put it in drive.

One. "Plateau" is not a valid reason, by itself, to end Medicare skilled nursing coverage. That has been true for thirteen years. A 2013 federal settlement called Jimmo v. Sebelius invalidated the old rule that said Medicare only pays if the patient is getting better. Medicare today has to cover skilled care that is needed to maintain the patient's condition or slow decline, even if the patient is not improving. If the SNF is using the word "plateau" as the reason for ending coverage, that reasoning has been outdated for over a decade.

Two. On the back of the pink form there is a phone number. In Washington, it is Livanta, at 1-877-588-1123. Call it. The expedited appeal is free, available 24 hours a day, seven days a week, and you do not need a lawyer. While the appeal is under review, Medicare coverage continues. That alone buys you about seventy-two hours.

Three. An adult family home in Burien is often the right next chapter at the plateau point, and the SNF discharge planner rarely spells that out, because it is not her job to spell it out. AFH private pay in our area is $5,500 to $8,500 a month, roughly a third to a half of what a King County SNF bed costs once Medicare stops paying. Washington's COPES Medicaid waiver covers AFH care after qualification. 1:3 staff ratio instead of 1:15. A private bedroom. The same caregiver who said good morning yesterday saying good morning tomorrow.

You are not behind. The letter just turned the lights on.

If the pink form landed on your table this week, call me. The number is on the home page. Even if our home is not the right fit for your mother, I will help you find the one that is.

**Post copy:**

Today, April 20, 2026, is the compliance cutover date for the updated Medicare Notice of Medicare Non-Coverage (CMS-10123) and Detailed Explanation of Non-Coverage (CMS-10124) stock at skilled nursing facilities, home health agencies, and inpatient rehab facilities. Worth flagging across the King County discharge planning and case management community, because the families we serve are still structurally under-informed on three points.

First, "plateau" language has not been a legally sufficient basis for ending Medicare skilled nursing coverage since the 2013 *Jimmo v. Sebelius* class-action settlement invalidated the improvement standard. Medicare must cover skilled care needed to maintain a patient's condition or slow decline, not only skilled care that produces measurable improvement. Thirteen years in, the settlement has still not fully replaced the clinical shorthand used at bedside conversations.

Second, the expedited NOMNC appeal process through Washington's BFCC-QIO (Livanta, 1-877-588-1123) is free, 24/7, and under-utilized by families. Filing stops the clock on Medicare coverage during the review and triggers a DENC with the SNF's written clinical reasoning within four hours. For families genuinely navigating a plateaued discharge, the DENC often clarifies the care picture in ways the bedside conversation does not.

Third, direct SNF-to-AFH transfers are frequently a better placement match than SNF custodial care at the plateau point. King County AFH private pay ranges $5,500 to $8,500 monthly versus $12,000 to $15,000 for a private-pay SNF bed after Medicare stops. Washington's COPES waiver covers AFH care after qualification. 1:3 staff ratio. Private bedroom. House, not building.

I am happy to be a resource for hospital case managers, SNF discharge planners, elder law attorneys, geriatric care managers, and Medicaid navigators working with a family inside a 48-hour NOMNC window in south King County. Referral is straightforward, and I will say no directly if our home is not the right level of care for the patient.
