## 1. FACEBOOK POST (Primary, adult children + seniors)

**Suggested image concept:** A soft still life shot in a kitchen. A cordless phone or cell phone face-down on a warm cream countertop. A half-full coffee mug beside it, steam gone, afternoon light low through the window. A stack of unopened mail, a pair of reading glasses, a Post-it with a phone number scribbled on it in blue pen. No people. The image says: this is the kitchen right after the call, the moment the panic starts, and nobody is here to help her think. Sage tea towel in the frame for brand thread, or a small terracotta-colored dish on the counter.

**Post copy:**

It is 4:07 on a Friday afternoon and you are standing in your kitchen in Burien with the phone still warm in your hand.

The hospital called. A nurse, or a case manager, or a social worker. They were kind, and they were fast, and they said something like, "Your mom is doing great, we are going to send her home Monday morning, can you tell us where she is going?"

You said, "Okay."

Then you hung up, and you looked at your stairs. And your bathtub. And your calendar on Monday. And you heard a sentence in your own head that does not go away easily.

"We are not ready."

I want to talk to the woman standing in that kitchen right now. I am the one who opens the door of a small adult family home in Burien when Friday calls go sideways, and I want to say a few things nobody at the hospital has time to say to you.

One. "Medically stable" is not the same as "ready to go home and live the way she did before." It only means not actively in danger if she leaves this bed. A week in a hospital bed costs a senior 10 to 15 percent of their muscle mass. Add new medications, disrupted sleep, a little delirium, and you are bringing home a person who is meaningfully weaker than the one who went in. You are not imagining it. You are observing something real, and you are allowed to say it out loud.

Two. You have more say inside this system than the 4pm call made it sound like. Ask the hospital for the Important Message from Medicare notice. In Washington you can appeal the discharge timing through Livanta, our state's Medicare Quality Improvement Organization, and while that review is happening the hospital cannot discharge her. Most families never hear about this. You are hearing about it now.

Three. This weekend is not a forever decision. A respite room for thirty days is a decision. A rehab stay is a decision. A small adult family home in Burien with a room open in seventy two hours is a decision. Every one of those is reversible, and every one of those buys you time to make a better one next month.

You are not behind. The hospital just turned the lights on.

If you are reading this at 11pm with the laptop open on the counter, you can call us. Even if we are not the right fit for your mom this week, I will personally help you figure out who is.

This is the single most expensive fact the hospital may not volunteer. Ask directly. Get the paper copy. Keep it.

## 4. LINKEDIN POST (healthcare professionals + referral network)

**Suggested image concept:** A clean, professional still life. A physical paper copy of the Medicare Outpatient Observation Notice form face-up on a desk, with a pen resting across it, a patient chart folder underneath, soft natural daylight. Alternately a warm candid of a case manager's hands mid-conversation, clinical but humanized.

**Post copy:**

Tomorrow, April 20, 2026, is the compliance date for the updated Medicare Outpatient Observation Notice (CMS-10611). Worth flagging across the discharge planning and case management network, because the families we work with are still under-informed about what "observation status" actually means for their parent's discharge picture.

The gap between observation and inpatient is not academic. It is the difference between Medicare Part A covering up to 100 days of skilled nursing after a qualifying three-night inpatient stay, and the family being handed a full-price SNF bill on a Monday they did not see coming.

Three things worth baking into family conversations this quarter:

First, the updated MOON requires both a written form and a verbal explanation of outpatient status. The verbal piece is where families actually absorb the information. It should not be reduced to a signature ritual in front of an exhausted adult daughter on hour forty seven.

Second, Washington families have appeal rights through Livanta that most bedside discharge conversations never surface. A clean referral to a small-home option only lands well if the family first understands the real timeline they are working inside.

Third, direct hospital-to-adult-family-home transfers are legal, fast, and frequently a better care match than SNF for patients who will not return to baseline and whose family is already navigating dementia or significant ADL needs.

At Burien Best Care Home, we can typically coordinate a Washington AFH admission within 24 to 72 hours of a discharge order when a private room is available. I am happy to be a resource for hospital case managers, SW teams, and discharge planners navigating a tight weekend for a family that needs a small-home, high-ratio care option in south King County.
