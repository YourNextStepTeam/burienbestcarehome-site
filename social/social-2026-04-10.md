You are not failing. You are running into the math.

Sundowning is a physiological spike that tends to hit between 4pm and 7pm in people with dementia. You cannot staff a three person hour with one exhausted person who loves the patient too much. That isn't a character flaw. That's math.

Just a conversation.

## 4. LinkedIn Post

**Audience:** Hospital discharge planners, geriatric social workers, primary care providers, King County healthcare referral network, adult protective services, elder law attorneys

---

**Post copy:**

A clinical observation from 20 years inside dementia care that I wish more discharge planners knew.

The hour at which a family calls us for the first time is almost never random. Roughly 70 percent of our first calls in the past year have come between 6pm and 10pm. Not because families are procrastinating during the day. Because the first call happens in the window immediately after sundowning has broken them.

There is a small but important reframe for our field here.

We tend to describe sundowning as a behavioral symptom of dementia. Environmental modification, routine stability, pharmacologic management where indicated, all standard. The missing frame is that sundowning is also, functionally, the moment at which home caregiving capacity becomes mathematically insufficient for the patient's needs.

The 4pm to 7pm symptom spike overlaps exactly with the period when the primary family caregiver's own cognitive and physical reserves are at their lowest, while competing demands (dinner, dependent children, household maintenance) are at their highest. This is not a moral or educational problem on the family's part. It is a staffing model limit.

For clinicians and discharge planners, this has two practical implications.

First, when a family presents with "I can't manage sundowning anymore," we should be hearing that as a structural capacity statement, not an emotional surrender. The intervention is not more education. It is a shift to a 24 hour staffing model.

Second, small 6 bed adult family homes in Washington State operate at a 1:3 caregiver to resident ratio, with staff rotation designed precisely to place a fresh caregiver into the 4pm to 8pm window. This is the structural match to the sundowning curve that large institutional memory care units often cannot achieve.
