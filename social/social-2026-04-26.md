## 1. FACEBOOK POST (Primary, adult children + seniors)

**Suggested image concept:** A close-up shot, slightly overhead, of a woman's hand resting on the edge of a Mother's Day card display. The cards in soft focus behind her hand, all in shades of pink, cream, and pale yellow. Visible card spines read "Mom," "Mother," "For Mom on Mother's Day." Her hand is bare, no rings, no manicure, just a real hand of a daughter in her late 40s. Soft drugstore fluorescent light, cool but not harsh. Anchoring color palette: warm cream (#FFF5EB) overall wash through color grading, sage green (#7D9B76) introduced through a blurred shopping basket strap on her wrist, soft terracotta (#C4856A) accent through a single cherry-blossom pink card front in foreground. The emotional center of this image is the stillness of her hand. She is not picking a card. She is not putting one back. She is just stopped. Plan B: a wide overhead shot of the same daughter walking out of the drugstore door with a basket containing only a bag of dog food and a prescription bag, no Mother's Day card. The empty space in the basket is the emotional center.

**Post copy:**

It is a Sunday afternoon in Burien. Two weeks until Mother's Day.

The daughter is in the Bartell Drugs on 152nd. She has a basket on her arm with a refill of her own prescription and a small bag of dog food. She promised herself this year she would not be the one who waited until the last week.

On her way to the register, she stops in front of the Mother's Day card wall.

She picks up the first card. "To my mother, who has always known just what to say."

She puts it back.

The next card says, "Mom, you taught me everything I know."

She puts that one back too.

The next one opens with, "Thanks for always being there."

She closes it carefully, like it might break.

Her mother has moderate dementia. Her mother lives in an adult family home now, down the road from her old house. Her mother sometimes forgets the names of her own grandchildren, and last Sunday she asked the daughter who she was while still patting her hand and smiling.

The cards on this rack were written for a version of her mother who is not the mother she has anymore.

If you are that daughter, standing in that aisle, with a basket of dog food and a prescription bag, two weeks out from a holiday you cannot quite figure out how to do this year, I want to tell you what twenty years in senior care in Burien has taught me about that drugstore aisle.

You are not failing at finding a card. There is no card on that rack written for the mother you actually have right now. They were written for the mother who could read them in 2014. They were written for a relationship phase that the disease has moved you out of. You are standing in front of a wall of inventory aimed at a daughter you used to be, looking for words for a daughter you have become, and there is a real grief in that gap.

It has a name. The clinical term is anticipatory grief. The Alzheimer's Association names it. The Family Caregiver Alliance teaches caregivers how to recognize it. HopeHealth describes it as the mourning that happens while your loved one is still here, the way you grieve the losses of memory and personality and the version of your relationship long before the actual death.

You are not being morbid. You are not jumping the gun. You are grieving on the schedule the disease has set for you. The drugstore card aisle is just where it shows up on a Sunday in late April with a bag of dog food in your basket.

So here is what I want you to know.

You do not have to find a card that fits.

You are allowed to write your own card on a blank piece of stationery. You are allowed to buy the silliest card on the rack, the one with cats wearing party hats, because she always loved cats and she will smile at it for thirty seconds and that is enough. You are allowed to skip the card entirely. You are allowed to walk out of the Bartell's with the dog food and the prescription bag and nothing else, get in your car, sit in the parking lot, and cry. That is also Mother's Day.

There are at least four Mother's Days available to you on May 10, and only one of them involves a card from the rack.

There is the brunch you bring to her in her room. Coffee from the place on 152nd. A pastry she can manage. Tulips in a small mason jar. Twenty minutes of you sitting next to her while she watches the light through the window.

There is the slow drive through Seahurst Park while the cherry blossoms are still hanging on. Her in the passenger seat. The same playlist of hymns or Patsy Cline or whatever she loved when you were small. Half an hour of motion and music and her hand on the door handle.

There is the photo album you turn the pages of together. You name the people she still recognizes. She names the ones you forgot. Forty minutes of her remembering one corner of her life out loud.

There is the day you stay home, let her caregivers handle Mother's Day so she still has a beautiful one, and you allow yourself the grief that the card aisle started for you two weeks early.

None of these require a card. All of them are Mother's Day.

If your mother is in a small adult family home this May, the holiday can actually happen for her. In a 6-bed home, the caregiver-to-resident ratio means she gets to wear the dress she loves and have her hair washed the morning of and have her favorite breakfast on a real plate before you get there. You walk in with flowers and you get to be the daughter again, not the event planner, not the hairdresser, not the one carrying the casserole and the grief at the same time.

If you are still in the in-between, still considering whether placement is the right step before Mother's Day or after it, please reach out. You can find me at burienbestcarehome.com. I am happy to sit with you on the phone or at our kitchen table and walk through what your specific Mother's Day could look like, in any of these arrangements, with no pitch and no pressure.

You do not have to find a card that fits. You are allowed to write your own. You are allowed to skip the card entirely. You are allowed to grieve in the Bartell's parking lot. That is also Mother's Day.

## 4. LINKEDIN POST (Healthcare professionals + referral network)

**Subject for the feed:** Anticipatory grief in the run-up to Mother's Day, and the small-home structural advantage on holiday execution.

**Post copy:**

Two weeks out from Mother's Day, the discharge planners and care managers in my Burien network start getting a particular kind of phone call.

It is not the usual placement question.

It is the daughter who placed her mother in March, who is now standing in the Bartell on 152nd in front of the card rack, who has just realized that the cards on the wall were written for a relationship phase the dementia has already moved her past, and who is calling to ask whether she should bring her mother home for Mother's Day.

This is the conversation I want my referral partners to be ready for in the next fourteen days.

The clinical name for what she is feeling is anticipatory grief. HopeHealth, the Alzheimer's Association, and the Family Caregiver Alliance all name it. It is the mourning that happens while the loved one is still physically present and cognitively or relationally absent. Holidays concentrate it. Mother's Day is its peak event for this audience.

What I have learned from twenty years of running this kind of placement work in King County is that the right answer to "should I bring her home" is almost never the binary question. It is a menu. There are at least four Mother's Days available to a family on May 10, and only one of them involves disrupting a stable placement.

The structural advantage of a 6-bed adult family home on a holiday is underrated. With a 1:3 caregiver-to-resident ratio, the holiday can actually be executed. Hair washed the morning of. Favorite dress laid out. Real breakfast on a real plate. The visiting daughter walks in with flowers and gets to be the daughter for two hours, not the event planner. The visit is restorative for both sides because the work has already been done.

If you are a hospital social worker, discharge planner, geriatric care manager, or hospice coordinator in the King County referral network, I want you to know two things this week:

One. The "should I bring her home for Mother's Day" call is a high-emotion moment. Treat it as anticipatory grief, not as a logistics question. The family does not need a checklist. They need a framework.

Two. We have respite slots and short-term Mother's Day weekend options at Burien Best Care Home if a family needs a structured path between "she stays where she is" and "I drive across town twice in one day." Reach out before May 1 if you have a family who would be served by this conversation.
