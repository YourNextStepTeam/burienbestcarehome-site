**Post copy (245 words):**

A note for colleagues in memory clinics, dementia training, and dementia-informed home care, for the next family who arrives at your door asking, "Should I tell Mom that Dad died, again."

Therapeutic fibbing is one of the most evidence-supported and least-talked-about standards in dementia communication, and it is one of the highest-leverage clinical conversations a memory clinic social worker or geriatric primary care physician can have with an adult child in the first six months after diagnosis.

Three clinical reframes I offer families who arrive in my kitchen in Burien:

First, the neurobiological reframe. In moderate to advanced dementia (Stage 5-6 on the Global Deterioration Scale), the hippocampal consolidation pathway is no longer intact for new long-term memory. Telling a parent that a spouse has died does not teach. It re-traumatizes. The family needs to hear this from a clinician, not infer it from a Reddit thread at midnight.

Second, the validation reframe. Naomi Feil's Validation Method and Teepa Snow's Positive Approach to Care are the two most widely adopted dementia communication frameworks in the United States. Both treat the emotional reality of the moment, not the factual reality of the calendar, as the legitimate target of care. The Alzheimer's Society UK and the Family Caregiver Alliance both publish family-facing guidance recommending therapeutic fibbing where it reduces suffering.

Third, the cognitive load reframe. Adult daughters carrying the weight of "should I tell her again today" experience a measurable form of accumulated re-traumatization that is invisible in standard caregiver burden inventories. Naming this directly with families, and explicitly giving them permission to fib, often unlocks a conversation about respite, home care augmentation, or AFH placement that has been stuck for months.

If helpful in your next family conversation, please use freely.

Washington families can reach the Alzheimer's Association Western and Central WA 24 hour helpline at 1-800-272-3900.
