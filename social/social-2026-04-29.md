**Post copy:**

It is a Wednesday afternoon in Burien. Eleven days until Mother's Day.

The daughter is parked in the small driveway of her mother's adult family home, on her lunch break. The engine is off. There is a folded cream cardigan with pearl buttons on the passenger seat, the one her mother asked for in February. There is a small bunch of grocery-store tulips on the floorboard. There is a cup of cold coffee in the cup holder. Her phone is face-down on the dashboard.

She has been parked here for nine minutes.

She has eighteen minutes left of her lunch break.

She has rehearsed the visit on the drive over the way she rehearsed dentist appointments when she was nine. What she is afraid of has a name. The name is not "she will be agitated." The name is not "she will cry." The name is "she will look at me politely, the way she used to look at strangers at the grocery store, and she will not know who I am."

If you are that daughter, sitting in your own parked car in your own driveway in Burien or White Center or Normandy Park or West Seattle eleven days before Mother's Day, before the visit you have not yet had the courage to begin, here is what twenty years in senior care has taught me.

Recognition and reception are not the same thing.

Recognition is the function of the brain that retrieves your name as a stored fact, the way it used to retrieve a phone number or a street address. That function is being taken by the disease. Some days it works. Some days it does not. Some weeks it works for the friend from church and not for you. Some Sundays it works backwards and she calls you by your aunt's name. Recognition is a moving target.

Reception is different. Reception is what happens when you walk in and your mother feels the cadence of your voice, the warmth of your hand, the scent on your sweater, the rhythm of how you sit down next to her on the couch. Reception is stored on a different shelf in the brain. That shelf empties last. In my experience over the years, that shelf is still full long after the name shelf is empty.

You did not drive here to be recognized. You drove here to be received.

That sentence is a permission, not a consolation. Hold it for a second.

Before you open the car door, here is the five-minute pre-arrival prep that changes the visit before it begins. This is for you, not for her.

One. Pause for one breath in the car. Let the steering wheel be cool under your hands for the length of one inhale and one exhale. You are not a child rushing into the house. You are a woman walking into a sanctuary. Walk in slow.

Two. Wear something she has smelled on you for thirty years. The same perfume from the bathroom counter at the old house. The same hand cream. The same laundry detergent if you have not switched. Scent goes through a different door in the brain than the front-door door. Scent reaches the limbic system without stopping at the name registry. She may know you by smell before she knows you by face.

Three. Carry a small scent-loaded object if you have one. The cedar sachet from her closet drawer. The cinnamon stick from the cookie tin. A small swatch of the lavender from the garden at the old house. Something she has held a thousand times. Set it on the table next to her chair. Do not announce it. Let the room remember her.

Four. Walk in with her name on your lips first. Announce yourself as a gift, not as a quiz. "Hi Mom, it is me, your daughter, it is Sarah. I came to see you. I brought your cardigan and some tulips." That sentence does the work three other sentences would have to do. It tells her who she is. It tells her who you are. It tells her why you came. You are doing for her, in two seconds, what her brain can no longer do for itself.

Skip "do you know who I am." That sentence is a test. Your mother is not in a moment to be tested. The test is the part that hurts. Take the test out of the visit. The visit gets warmer the second you do.

Five. Sit at her eye level. Not standing over her. Not from across the room. Sit on the arm of the couch next to her chair, or pull the small chair over so your face is at her face. Take her hand. Hands carry more recognition than faces do, in my experience. The shape of your particular hand on hers is a thirty-year archive your mother's body still knows.

Then, with all of that done, walk in.

If she lights up, the visit is a gift. If she calls you by the wrong name, the visit is still a gift. If she asks you who you are with the polite hostess voice, you tell her again. "It is me, Mom, it is Sarah, I am your daughter, I came to see you." Say it without stress in your voice. Say it the way you would tell a friend the time. The information is a kindness.

Stay twenty minutes. Twelve minutes is also fine. Eight minutes is fine. There is no minimum. There is no medal for endurance. Leave when she gets tired. Leave before she gets tired if you can. Short, warm, repeatable visits are better than one long visit you have to recover from for two days.

If your mother is in a small adult family home, this part actually works in your favor. In a 6-bed home, the caregiver who has been with your mother for the past four months knows your face and your name. She announces you. "Mary, your daughter Sarah is here, look up, your daughter Sarah brought your tulips." That announcement is itself a memory aid. The chair your mother is sitting in is the same chair as last time. The lap blanket is the same blanket. The smell from the kitchen is the same soup that was on the stove the last three Wednesdays. Continuity is the cheapest, most powerful memory cue dementia care has, and a small home is built on continuity. There is no fluorescent corridor to disorient her in the first thirty seconds. There is no nurse station to register at. There is just a real living room with a real couch and your mother in her chair, and you walking in with tulips.

Here is the thing nobody told me when I was first walking my own family through this. Eleven days before Mother's Day, sitting in a parked car with cellophane sweating against your floorboard, the bravest sentence you can say to yourself is not "she will recognize me." The bravest sentence is "it is okay if she does not."

She does not have to remember you to receive you. The version of her that recognizes you on this Sunday in May may or may not show up. The version of her that loves you is in the chair by the window. Both versions are still her. The Mother's Day visit is not an exam. It is a witness. You did not come to be remembered. You came to love her on a Sunday in May.

If you are still in the parked car, still nine minutes in, still rehearsing, you can find me at burienbestcarehome.com. I am happy to sit with you on the phone or at our kitchen table here in Burien and walk through what your visit could look like. We can rehearse the doorway. We can rehearse the first sentence. We can rehearse the moment she might not know your name. You do not have to do this part alone.

Eleven days. Cellophane on the floorboard. Cardigan with pearl buttons on the passenger seat. Take one breath. Walk in slow. Say her name first.

She is in there. And she will receive you. That is not nothing. That is the whole reason you came.

Five-minute pre-arrival prep. Before you open the car door. Pause one breath. Wear what she has smelled on you for thirty years. Walk in with her name on your lips first. Skip "do you know who I am." Sit at her eye level. Take her hand. Then walk in slow.

**5/5**
She does not have to remember you to receive you. The version of her that recognizes you may or may not show up. The version of her that loves you is in the chair by the window. Both are still her. Walk in slow. burienbestcarehome.com
