**Today's theme:** The sole family caregiver. Sibling absence as a form of unconscious self-protection. Asking for specific help (with a dollar amount or a calendar date). Permission to stop waiting for fairness. Permission to stop sanitizing the reports.

**Community research snapshot:** Today's content was seeded from four live conversations. On r/AgingParents and the Alzheimer's Society Dementia Support Forum, the question "why won't my siblings help" is an evergreen thread (not a one-off), which tells us it is a structural family pattern, not a situational problem. AgingCare.com's perennial "3 Excuses From Siblings Who Don't Help With Aging Parents" article is still one of their highest-read pieces because the three excuses (I live too far, I'm too busy, I never got along with Mom anyway) are nearly universal. And the Caregiver Action Network resource on emotional caregiving continues to reframe guilt as "regret about being human and making difficult decisions," which is the exact permission language sole caregivers need.

**Post copy:**

Here is the thing nobody tells you about being the adult child doing all of it.

The hardest part is not the bathing, or the medication management, or the middle of the night phone calls. The hardest part is the group text from the sibling in another city that says, "thinking about Mom, let me know how she's doing."

You stare at it. You are too tired to answer. You are also too tired to be angry about it. You just put the phone face down on the counter, because you don't trust yourself to say what you actually want to say.

If that is your life right now, I want to tell you something I have learned from twenty years inside Burien and King County families.

You are not imagining the pattern. More than half of all eldercare in this country ultimately falls to one person, usually a daughter, usually in her late forties, usually doing sixty to ninety percent of the work. Siblings contribute sporadically, or financially, or not at all. This is not because you are "the responsible one." It is a pattern researchers see across every income level, every culture, every religion, and every family size.

And here is the first principles truth that most "get your siblings to step up" advice skips over.

Your siblings are not being lazy. They are in a quiet, protective form of denial, because what you are asking them to do is exactly what they are structurally avoiding. Witnessing the decline. Facing their own mortality. Sitting in a room that smells like a nursing home and watching the person who raised them not recognize them. Their absence is not disrespect. It is a very expensive form of self-protection. You cannot negotiate a person out of denial with a better text message.

That does not mean you stop asking. It means you stop waiting.

Two things that actually work, from what I see in real Burien kitchens.

One, ask for specific help in writing, with a dollar amount or a calendar date attached. "Would you cover Mom's prescription copays this month, $186, Venmo is fine?" gets a real answer. "Can you help more" does not.

Two, stop protecting your siblings from the truth. Stop sanitizing the reports. "Mom had a rough week" is the sentence you use when the honest version is "Mom fell twice, called me by my dead aunt's name, soiled herself four times, and I haven't slept more than three hours in a row since Tuesday." Your siblings cannot respond to a version of reality you are hiding from them.

And if you have not slept through the night in two weeks, if you are starting to resent the person you love most, if you cried in a parking lot this week, please hear me. That is data. It is telling you that the math of home care is no longer survivable. It is not telling you that you are failing.

If you are in Burien or King County and you are the one carrying this, I wrote a letter for you today at burienbestcarehome.com/blog. Or come sit in our kitchen, drink a cup of tea, and ask questions without any expectation. No pressure. No sales pitch. Just a conversation with a human who has watched a lot of families find their way through this exact hour.

You are not failing. You are the one who showed up. That counts for more than you know.

With care,
Becca

## 4. LinkedIn Post

**Audience:** Hospital discharge planners, geriatric social workers, elder law attorneys, primary care and geriatric medicine, adult protective services, King County healthcare referral network, WA State ADS (Aging and Disability Services)

---

**Post copy:**

A clinical pattern from 20 years inside family care that I think our field underweights.

When a family member calls us for the first time about placing a parent in residential care, in roughly 80 percent of cases there are other living siblings, and in roughly 80 percent of those cases only one sibling is actively caregiving. The distribution is not random and it is not a function of geography. It is a predictable family systems pattern that shows up across income levels, cultures, and family sizes.

2026 survey data continues to bear this out. 49 percent of family caregivers report high burden. 35 percent report clinically significant anxiety. 33 percent report depression. These numbers are concentrated, not evenly distributed, in the subgroup that carries the work alone.

The reframe I would offer colleagues in discharge planning, geriatric care management, and elder law is this. The non-participating siblings in these cases are almost never disengaged out of malice or laziness. They are in a protective form of anticipatory grief avoidance. They are, functionally, refusing to witness what the primary caregiver is already witnessing. This is an important distinction clinically, because it changes the intervention.

When we approach the family system with "how do we get the other siblings to do more," we are often intervening against a dynamic that will not yield, because we are asking the non-participating siblings to break their own defenses. When we instead approach it with "how do we reduce the primary caregiver's load to something sustainable," we get traction, because we are working with the grief architecture instead of against it.

Two practical implications for our referral conversations with families.

First, the decision to move a parent into residential care is rarely a family consensus. It is almost always the sole caregiver saying yes, and the absent siblings saying neither yes nor no, which the primary caregiver often reads as rejection when it is actually their own grief speaking in a different language. Naming this for the family in the first conversation can reduce guilt dramatically.

Second, 6 bed adult family homes in Washington state are specifically structured to carry the load the sole caregiver cannot sustain. 1:3 staffing ratios. Medicaid participation. Private care suites. Staff continuity that supports the kind of relationship-based memory care that larger facilities often cannot staff for. For the discharge planner managing a sole caregiver in crisis, an adult family home is frequently the right-sized match.

Burien Best Care Home serves King County families navigating this transition. If you have a patient, client, or family member whose primary caregiver has hit the sibling wall, I welcome a conversation about whether we might be the right fit. Referrals and tours are always at no cost.
