It is Friday afternoon in Burien, four-thirty, the first day of May. Mother's Day is nine days from now. Your kitchen smells like the coffee you reheated at three. Your mother is in her recliner in the back room with the television on a channel she did not choose, and the volume is louder than it needs to be, because she cannot find the remote and you stopped looking for it after the third hour today. The yellow legal pad in front of you has six things written on it, four of them crossed out, two of them just sitting there, looking back at you. The phone is face-down on the table because if you turn it over you will have to see whether your brother has texted back, and he has not.

I want to tell you something that I think you already know but have not yet said out loud, even to yourself, sitting at this table with this cup of coffee that has been three temperatures today and is now, again, cold.

You cannot do another Mother's Day like last year.

Last year you hosted in your dining room because she was too disoriented to ride to a restaurant. You set the table with the linens she gave you when you got married. You roasted the chicken the way she taught you. You cued up the Roberta Flack record, because that record was the closest thing you could find in the house to a version of her you could still find. She did not eat. She asked, three times, where her husband was. He has been gone four years. You answered the first time with the truth, which made her cry. You answered the second time with a softer version of the truth, which made her angry. You answered the third time with something that was not quite a lie but was not quite the truth, and she did not believe you, and the kids stopped eating, and your husband took the chicken off the table because nobody was eating, and you sat in the kitchen for forty-five minutes after they all went home, looking at the dishes, not crying, because you did not have anything left for crying. That was last Mother's Day. That is the Mother's Day you are nine days out from doing again.

You are not going to do it again. You already know that. The question on this kitchen table on this Friday afternoon is not whether you can do another year of caregiving alone. The question is whether you make the call before Sunday May 10 or after.

I am going to tell you why before is the answer.

If you make the call after Mother's Day, you will spend the next nine days white-knuckling the version of the day you already know is going to break you, and you will spend the day itself either failing in front of your family or holding it together by holding your breath, and you will spend the Monday morning after Mother's Day, May 11, doing the same kitchen table search you are doing right now, except with one more day of your own depletion underneath you. The same six things will be on the legal pad. Two of them will still not be crossed out. Your brother still will not have texted back.

If you make the call before Mother's Day, you do not have to have placed her by Sunday. You do not have to tour anywhere by Sunday. You do not have to tell her anything by Sunday. All you have to do is stop being the only person carrying the question. You make one phone call to one operator who has been doing this in Burien for twenty years, and the question stops sitting on top of you and starts sitting next to you, where someone else can also see it. Then on Sunday, May 10, you do whatever version of Mother's Day your nervous system can hold. Roast a chicken or do not roast a chicken. Set the table or do not set the table. Have the kids over for thirty minutes or have them not come at all this year. The day will still be hard. The day after will not be the same day it was going to be.

I am Becca. I run an adult family home in Burien. I am the woman on the other end of the call you are deciding whether to make this afternoon. I have been doing this for twenty years. I am the one who picks up the phone when the daughter on the other end is not crying and not exactly calm either, just tired in a way that has stopped having a sound. You do not need a list of questions. You do not need to know the difference between an adult family home and assisted living. You do not need to have looked anything up first. You can call to ask one question, or to cry for two minutes, or to find out whether anyone in Burien has a private room and a chair by a west-facing window that might one day be your mother's chair. None of those calls is the wrong call. All of them are the call.

You are not abandoning her. You are choosing a version of love that is bigger than what one body can carry. The version of you that hosted last year, that roasted the chicken the way she taught you, that played the Roberta Flack record because that record was the closest thing you could find to a version of her, that version of you was love. The version of you who is sitting at this kitchen table on this Friday afternoon with a yellow legal pad and a cold cup of coffee, who is admitting that one body cannot keep doing what two bodies barely did, that version of you is also love. They are the same love. The shape just changed.

If you are anywhere in Burien, White Center, Normandy Park, West Seattle, or anywhere in South King County, and Mother's Day is sitting on your kitchen table this afternoon, please make the call before Sunday. Not because the call solves the day. Because the call lets you stop being the only person carrying the day.

The chair by the west-facing window has not been promised to anyone yet.

A note for the King County referral network on the Friday before Mother's Day weekend, May 1, 2026.

The pre-placement crisis window is most clinically active in the seventy-two hours before a major holiday. The adult daughter who has been carrying the at-home dementia caregiving load alone, often through the second year of decline and frequently in the absence of meaningful sibling involvement, will use the holiday calendar as a forcing function. We see the same pattern every year. Phone volume to small Burien adult family homes spikes Friday afternoon to Saturday morning the weekend before Mother's Day, and again on the Tuesday after. The Tuesday-after caller is the daughter who white-knuckled the day. The Friday-before caller is the daughter who decided to stop white-knuckling.

The Friday-before call is the higher-leverage call. It is also the more fragile one. It often arrives without prepared questions, without a tour history, without sibling alignment, and without a plan for how to talk to the resident. The most useful intake response is not a brochure. The most useful intake response is permission to make the call without preparing, and a clear acknowledgment that one phone call does not equal a placement.

For King County colleagues working with families in the second-year-at-home caregiving window, particularly those whose caregivers are presenting in primary care with sleep disruption, weight change, or anxiety symptoms in late April and early May, I have a one-page handout titled *"What the Call Before Mother's Day Actually Has To Be."* It is written for the family member, not the operator. It is designed to lower the activation energy of the first call to any small home in the South King County area. Reach out anytime if you would like a copy for your intake folders or family education packets.
