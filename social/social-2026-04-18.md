# Social Content, April 18, 2026

Theme: The visit is not the hard part. The last ten minutes are. The driveway freeze at 3:47 on a Sunday. The goodbye at the door that she never quite survives. The crying that is not a sign the home is wrong, it is a sign her attachment is working. The reframe that the visit is presence, not performance. The goodbye framework that actually softens the departure. Twenty to forty minutes. Hand off to the caregiver out loud. Don't say goodbye at the door. Say it in her chair.

## 1. FACEBOOK POST (Primary, adult children + seniors)

**Suggested image concept:** A warm afternoon interior, shot from the perspective of a chair by a window. A woman's hand, elderly, resting on the armrest of a living room chair in soft light. A younger hand covering it, briefly, in a goodbye gesture. No faces. A lamp turned on beside them. A folded paper on the side table. A sage throw. Warm cream walls. The composition says: this is what a goodbye actually looks like, and it happens in the chair, not at the door. Brand thread in sage or terracotta.

**Post copy:**

It is 3:47 on a Sunday afternoon. She is in her car in the driveway of a small house in Burien with a camellia bush out front. In the passenger seat she has a canvas tote. A ziplock of her mother's favorite shortbread. A folder of printed photos from Walgreens. A cardigan she found at the back of the upstairs closet because the evenings are still cool. She has been sitting in the car for eleven minutes.

She is not afraid of the visit.

She is afraid of the last ten minutes of the visit.

Three weeks ago her mother moved in. The first two visits were fine. The third visit, her mother cried when she stood up to leave and said please do not go, and the daughter drove home on the 509 with her hands shaking on the wheel. Now she is in this driveway instead, one more week in, trying to decide whether to go in for an hour or for twenty minutes, whether to say goodbye or just slip out while her mother is distracted, which feels like a betrayal, and whether that is better or worse than the crying.

This is the part nobody talks about. The visits are not the hard part. The architecture around the visits is.

I want to say three things, because I am the woman who opens the door when she finally gets out of the car.

One. When your mother cries at the goodbye, that is not evidence that the home is wrong. It is evidence that your mother's attachment system is intact. She knows who you are. She feels the loss of you leaving. In the dementia care literature this is called a contact reaction, and it is so common in the first two to four months after placement that the caregivers at good homes build their workflow around it.

Two. Do not say goodbye at the door. Say it in her chair. Leave your coat and bag in the car before you even come in so there is no bag-zip, no coat-rustle, nothing her body recognizes as the leaving cue. Kiss her forehead. Hold her hand for a beat. Stand up without commentary. Do not pause at the threshold to look back. The pause is for you, not for her, and it costs her more than it gives you.

Three. Tell the caregiver you are leaving, out loud, where your mother can hear it. "I am heading out, thanks for the coffee." Your mother is not being left alone. She is being handed off. That is the softest landing for a departure I have ever seen work.

The crying usually softens on its own around month three. Until then, the architecture of the goodbye matters more than any single visit.

If you are sitting in a driveway in Burien right now and the camellia bush is out front, come on in. The coffee is on.

## 4. LINKEDIN POST (Healthcare professionals + referral network)

**Audience:** Hospital discharge planners, geriatric care managers, dementia care nurses, primary care and hospitalist teams, A Place for Mom and Concierge Care Advisors referral partners across King County.

**Suggested image:** Warm afternoon living room interior, a chair by a window, a lamp turned on beside it, a folded paper on the arm. No people. Soft natural light. Lived-in. Sage or terracotta accent for brand thread.

**Post copy:**

A clinical observation from a Burien adult family home for discharge planners, geriatric care managers, and dementia care teams counseling families through weeks three to eight of placement.

The single most common reason families second-guess a placement decision in the first two months is not quality of care, medication management, or cost. It is a departure event. The parent cries when the adult child stands up to leave. The adult child interprets the crying as evidence that the home is wrong, drives home in emotional crisis, and spends the next seventy two hours researching alternate placements.

The clinical frame matters here. In the dementia care literature, a departure cry at the goodbye is called a contact reaction. It is an attachment signal, not a placement signal. The parent is recognizing the adult child, registering the departure in real time, and expressing affect appropriately. In the first two to four months after placement, contact reactions are so common they are the baseline, not the exception. They typically soften by month three as the facility becomes the parent's primary home environment and the adult child becomes a visitor rather than the anchor attachment.

Two structural variables predictably reduce contact reaction intensity at the goodbye. First, departure architecture. The adult child who leaves coat and bag in the car, says goodbye seated in the parent's chair rather than at the door, and explicitly hands the parent off to a named caregiver out loud will see far less escalation than the adult child who announces "I need to go" from the threshold with a bag zipping shut. Second, caregiver continuity at the moment of departure. In a six-bed adult family home, the caregiver present at the goodbye is almost always someone the parent has known since week one, which produces a hand-off that closes the attachment loop rather than opening it. In large facilities with 1:10 or 1:20 staffing, the caregiver present at the departure is often a stranger to the parent on that shift, which does not close the loop.

The practical referral implication for families in the contact-reaction phase is not "visit less" or "visit more." It is counseling on departure architecture and selecting a care model where the hand-off at goodbye is structurally available.
