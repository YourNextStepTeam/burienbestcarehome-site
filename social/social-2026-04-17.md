# Social Content, April 17, 2026

Theme: What do they actually do all day. A good day at a small adult family home is not a cruise ship itinerary. It is rhythm, not schedule. It is household, not program. The real question inside the activities question is the existential one: will my parent still feel like a person. The answer is not a calendar. It is a kitchen.

## 1. FACEBOOK POST (Primary, adult children + seniors)

**Suggested image concept:** Real late-afternoon kitchen scene. Sunlight slanted through a side window. A folded newspaper on the table. A half-full coffee mug. A small basket of folded hand towels. A crossword, partly done, with a pencil resting on top. No people in the frame. The composition says: a household is mid-breath here, nobody is performing, this is just a real afternoon. Soft sage napkin or terracotta ceramic somewhere for brand color thread. Warm, lived-in, not staged.

**Post copy:**

There is a moment that happens in almost every family in the first two or three weeks after a parent moves into care. The daughter is at her desk in a co-working space in Seattle at 2:47 on a Tuesday afternoon. She has three browser tabs open and a cold cup of coffee going filmy at the edge. She is supposed to be finishing a slide for work. What she is actually doing is trying to picture her mother.

Her mother moved into a small adult family home in Burien eleven days ago. The tour went well. The room has a window that looks out on a yard with a camellia bush. But she cannot hold a mental image of what her mother is doing right now, at this hour, in a house she has been inside of three times. So she calls at 6. Her mother says the day was fine. Her mother cannot remember what she had for lunch. The daughter goes home, feeds her kids, puts them to bed, and at 11:15pm she types the same six words into her laptop that every family types in those first few weeks:

*What do they actually do all day.*

I want to answer that, honestly. I am the woman on the other side of that camellia bush.

The picture in your head is probably wrong, but not for the reason most websites will tell you. Most websites will hand you back a calendar. Bingo Tuesdays, Armchair Yoga Thursdays, a field trip to the arboretum every other Friday. That calendar is designed to reassure you, but it is not the answer you actually came for. The question you are really asking is not logistical. It is existential. You are asking: will my mother still feel like a person.

A calendar does not answer that. A kitchen does.

At a small home, there are six residents. On any given weekday in the afternoon, some are napping, some are sitting at the kitchen table, someone is snapping beans for dinner because she always snapped beans, someone is folding towels at the dining table because she always folded towels, someone is watching a show with the volume just a touch too loud. The caregiver is nearby, not hovering, starting soup, flipping through the mail. There is no PA system. There is no activity director with a clipboard. There is a house, being lived in, with your mother inside of it.

That is what a good afternoon looks like. It is not built out of activities. It is built out of rhythm.

Here is the part that matters most. Published research on long-term care found that residents in large facilities spend as much as 47.5 percent of the day doing nothing, and 62.5 percent report being bored. That is what most families are quietly afraid of when they ask "what will she do all day." They are afraid of the empty stare at 3pm. A small home does not fix that with a bigger activity calendar. It fixes it structurally, by being a household instead of a program. The dishwasher needs loading. The laundry needs folding. The table needs setting. The mailman comes. These are not activities. They are life, and a small home lets your parent stay inside of them.

Napping is not the opposite of engagement, by the way. Napping in a room full of household sounds, after lunch, with people nearby, is a perfectly human afternoon. It is what most of us will want when we are eighty six.

If you want to know what your parent's afternoon actually looks like, come at 3pm on a Wednesday. Not 10am when everything is tidy. 3pm, when the light has slanted, when lunch is done, when the house is just being itself. That is the hour that tells you the truth.

The full piece is on the blog today. If you want to come see a real Wednesday afternoon in Burien, we are here.

## 4. LINKEDIN POST (Healthcare professionals + referral network)

**Audience:** Hospital discharge planners, geriatric care managers, primary care and hospitalist teams, social workers, A Place for Mom / Concierge Care Advisors referral partners, dementia care nurses across King County.

**Suggested image:** A real weekday afternoon kitchen in a home setting. Folded newspaper, half-full coffee mug, pencil on a crossword, hand towels in a basket on the counter, sunlight through a side window. No people. Lived-in. Not institutional. Soft sage or terracotta accent for brand thread.

**Post copy:**

A referral observation from a Burien adult family home, for discharge planners, geriatric care managers, and primary care teams sending families into the "what do they actually do all day" phase of placement.

The first two to four weeks after placement are when families are most likely to second guess the decision, and the single most common reason is that they do not have a mental model for how their parent is spending daytime hours. They call at 6pm. The parent cannot remember what she had for lunch. The adult child goes to bed at 11 with a knot in her stomach and types "what do they do all day" into Google.

The senior living industry has trained families to equate "activities" with "quality of care." Published research on everyday life in residential long-term care suggests the opposite. Residents in large facilities spend as much as 47.5 percent of the day doing nothing, and 62.5 percent report being bored, with 18.5 percent describing boredom as a constant condition. The activity-calendar model is a consequence of scale. At 60 to 120 residents, time has to be filled structurally, which is why programmed activities fill the brochure and unstructured hours fill the day.

The small home model under Washington State DSHS AFH licensure is capped at six residents and typically runs 1:3 staffing through the waking day. The practical referral implication is not "more activities." It is different physics. A household rhythm replaces a programmed schedule. The kitchen is the center, not the activities room. Caregivers know a resident's history within the first week, not the first quarter. Sensory presence, which is the appropriate engagement model for moderate to advanced dementia, is structurally available in a small home in a way it is not in a large facility.

For King County families where the question is not "can she be cared for" but "will she still feel like a person," the AFH model is often the cleaner clinical fit, especially for residents with cognitive impairment who do not tolerate a programmed day.

We accept Medicaid, serve short term and respite care, and work closely with discharge planners across South King County.
