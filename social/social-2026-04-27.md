**Post copy:**

It is a Saturday morning in Burien. Two weeks before Mother's Day.

The local sister is in the cell-phone lot at SEA. Alaska 1438 from Phoenix landed eight minutes ago. Her brother is somewhere between the jet bridge and baggage claim. She has been bracing for this visit for three weeks.

The drive from the cell-phone lot at SEA back to the adult family home in Burien is ten minutes if Highway 99 is forgiving. She has rehearsed those ten minutes in her head every night this week.

Here is what she is afraid of.

She is not afraid that her brother is going to see how much their mother has declined. She has been keeping him updated. She has sent him the neurologist's note. She has told him about the placement.

She is afraid of showtiming.

If you are a dementia caregiver, you already know that word. If you are not, here is the short version. Showtiming is the well-documented phenomenon where a person with dementia rallies for a short, novel, high-stimulation visit and presents a thirty-minute version of themselves that looks almost like the version of them from years ago. They make eye contact. They use names. They tell a story you have not heard them tell in months. And then the visitor leaves, and they are tired for the next two days, and they cannot remember who came.

She is afraid her brother is going to get thirty minutes of showtiming. She is afraid he is going to fly back to Phoenix on Sunday night and tell their cousins she over-reacted. She is afraid he is going to call her on Monday and ask, gently, whether the placement was premature. She is afraid the version of their mother who shows up for thirty minutes is going to become the version of their mother he carries home as the truth.

If you are that sister, sitting in your car at SEA or in your driveway anywhere in King County this Mother's Day weekend, here is what twenty years in Burien senior care has taught me about the next ninety minutes.

Showtiming is not your mother lying. It is not your brother seeing the truth. It is not you exaggerating. It is a neurological feature of the disease. Novelty and brief social stimulation activate patterns of social presentation that look like fluency. When the novelty ends, the patterns end. There is no version of this where you are wrong. There is no version of this where he is wrong. You are looking at the same mother on different time scales.

You are allowed to brace.

You are allowed to sit in the cell-phone lot for ten minutes before you pull forward. You are allowed to send him a one-paragraph orientation text before he gets in the car. Something like, "She will probably look better than you expect for the first half hour. She does that for new people. Please do not take that as the baseline. Ask the caregiver who has been with her for six weeks what 5pm on a Tuesday looks like, not what 11am on a Saturday looks like." You are allowed to ask the caregivers at the home to be honest with him about her last two weeks, even if your mother performs beautifully while he is in the room.

If your mother is in a small adult family home, this part actually works. In a 6-bed home, your brother will meet the same caregiver who has been with her for six weeks, by name, who can answer his questions in front of him, who can describe what she looks like at 5pm on a Tuesday and at 3am when she calls out for her own mother. He does not have to take your word for it. He gets to take a caregiver's word for it, in person, in the kitchen, while the kettle is on. That is one of the structural advantages of small-home care that nobody puts on the brochure.

You are also allowed to skip the Sunday airport drop-off if you need to. You are allowed to ask him to take an Uber back to SEA. You are allowed to spend Mother's Day afternoon in your own kitchen alone, after he leaves, and grieve the fact that your brother is still grieving a version of your mother that you grieved a year ago. He has not caught up. He will. The visit is part of how he will. You do not have to walk him through it the entire time.

If you are still in the in-between, still considering whether placement is the right step before Mother's Day or after it, please reach out. You can find me at burienbestcarehome.com. I am happy to sit with you on the phone or at our kitchen table and walk through what your specific Mother's Day weekend could look like, including the part where your out-of-town sibling lands at SEA and you have ten minutes on Highway 99 to set the tone.

He is going to see thirty minutes of her best self. You have seen six months of her hardest days. Both of you are looking at the same mother. Neither of you is wrong. You do not have to win the argument. You only have to not abandon what you know.

## 4. LINKEDIN POST (healthcare professionals + referral network)

The Mother's Day weekend visit from the out-of-town adult child is one of the most under-discussed clinical events in dementia caregiving.

We talk about it as a family dynamic. It is also a clinical observation window. And, handled carelessly, it is a structural threat to a fragile new placement.

Showtiming, the brief preserved social presentation that surfaces during short novel visits, is well-documented in the dementia literature. It is not deception by the resident. It is not exaggeration by the primary caregiver. It is a feature of the disease. The novelty of an adult child who has not been present recently is precisely the stimulation that activates it.

The clinical risk: the out-of-town sibling, often a secondary or absent decision-maker, takes a thirty-minute observation as the baseline, returns home, and begins, quietly or loudly, to question the placement decision. In families that have just moved a parent into residential care, this destabilizes the primary caregiver, the new placement, and the trust between siblings, all at once.

For those of us in the Burien and King County referral network, I want to suggest a small intervention that actually changes outcomes.

When a primary family caregiver mentions an out-of-town sibling visit on a holiday weekend, name showtiming for them. Send them the orientation language. Encourage them to introduce the sibling to the consistent caregiver, not just the resident. Encourage a stay long enough to see the rally end. In small home settings, including 6-bed adult family homes, this introduction is structurally available because the caregiver-to-resident ratio supports it. In larger facilities, it is harder, and clinicians can advocate for it explicitly.

Mother's Day on May 10 is thirteen days out. Many of the families on your caseloads are about to walk through this exact weekend.

If you serve dementia families in King County and want a one-page handout I am drafting on the out-of-town sibling visit and showtiming, message me. I am happy to share it with hospital social workers, hospice intake, geriatric care managers, and memory clinic teams across Providence Hospice of Seattle, EvergreenHealth, UW Memory and Brain Wellness Center, and Virginia Mason Franciscan.
