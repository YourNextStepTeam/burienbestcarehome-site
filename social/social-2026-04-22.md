## 1. FACEBOOK POST (Primary, adult children + seniors)

**Suggested image concept:** A Thursday afternoon still life. Soft diagonal afternoon light falling across a warm wood side table. A small paper towel loosely wrapped around a handful of cherry blossom sprigs, petals starting to scatter on the wood. A folded cream or sage cardigan at the edge of the frame. The composition stays the same: afternoon light, a gift meant for someone, an empty chair in the distance.

**Post copy:**

It is a Thursday afternoon in April. The cherry trees along 16th Avenue SW are dropping petals onto the sidewalk like confetti from a parade no one announced. You brought your mother a small bouquet of them, wrapped in a paper towel, because she used to cut cherry blossoms from the tree in your front yard in Normandy Park every single spring of your childhood.

You walk into the small house in Burien where she has lived for four months now.

She is in the rocker by the window with the afternoon light on her hair. She looks up, smiles politely, and says, "Hi there, dear. Are you new here? You have kind eyes."

You are forty three years old. You have been known by this woman since the moment you were born. And you are standing in the doorway holding cherry blossoms and you cannot speak.

I am writing this for the woman in that doorway.

My name is Becca Pitts. I run a six-bed adult family home in Burien. I have watched this exact scene more than a hundred times from my kitchen, and every single time I feel it in my chest like it is the first time. Here is what I want you to know tonight, before you close the laptop and go to bed.

What you are feeling has a name. It is called ambiguous loss, and a researcher named Pauline Boss gave us that language back in the 1970s. It is the particular grief that happens when a person is physically present but psychologically absent. With a death, the world knows what to do. Casseroles arrive. Coworkers sign cards. With ambiguous loss, nobody brings anything, because nobody knows she is gone yet. She is sitting at the kitchen table. She has kind eyes. She is also gone. Both sentences are true at the same time, and your brain does not know how to hold them at once, so it flips back and forth between them and exhausts you.

You are not failing her.

The version of your mother who knew your name is gone. You are allowed to mourn her. You have to mourn her, because grief that does not get a ceremony tends to sit inside the body for years.

And here is the thing almost nobody tells you. The point of the visit is not whether your mother remembers the visit. Cognitive memory and emotional memory live in different parts of the brain, and they do not fail at the same rate. Your mother may not remember that you were there. Her nervous system will hold the calm of your presence for hours after you leave. That hour was real while it was happening. Forgetting does not erase it from having existed.

You are not a long-lost daughter tonight. You are a beloved friend. Walk in warmly. Say your name gently, without the weight of her failure to produce it. Sit beside her in the sunlight, not across from her. Bring something that smells like home. Tell her what is happening right now. When she drifts into an old year, go with her. Stay thirty minutes. Leave while she is still smiling.

You are not failing her. You are learning a new way to love her.

She will not remember that you came. But for one hour on a Thursday afternoon in Burien, with cherry blossoms on the table, she was safe. She was warm. She was loved.

That hour was real.

If you would like a longer conversation about what memory care looks like inside a small adult family home, I am here. You can reach me at burienbestcarehome.com. No sales pitch. Just a kitchen table and a cup of tea.

**Post copy:**

A clinical and referral-network note for King County neurologists, primary care physicians, hospital case managers, geriatric care managers, dementia-specialized therapists, hospice social workers, and memory care intake staff.

Three observations from the kitchen of a six-bed adult family home in Burien, all of which are well established in the dementia-care literature but still rarely named at the point of care, and all of which materially change family coping when clinicians name them early.

First, ambiguous loss is the grief modality most family caregivers of a parent with dementia are actually living inside, and most of them do not know the term exists. Pauline Boss's 1970s scholarship on ambiguous loss, her 2011 book on dementia caregiving specifically, and the Alzheimer's Society of Canada's widely used "Ambiguous Loss and Grief in Dementia" family resource all converge on a simple clinical move: naming the grief modality out loud at the point of diagnosis or placement measurably reduces secondary caregiver morbidity. A two-minute explanation at the 3-month post-diagnosis visit, or at SNF discharge, or at AFH intake, is one of the lowest-cost and highest-yield interventions in the dementia-caregiving arc. Most families have never heard the phrase.

Second, the cognitive-memory versus emotional-memory distinction is not a comfort story, it is a finding. University of Iowa lesion studies and the larger body of subsequent work on hippocampal versus amygdala contributions to memory in neurodegenerative disease support the clinical observation that emotional tone of visits persists in residents with significant cognitive impairment for hours after the visit ends. This is the single most durable reframe for family members who have stopped visiting because "she does not know me anyway." Articulating this finding at the care conference, in plain language, reliably restarts visit cadence in families who have drifted into avoidance.

Third, the architecture of a six-bed adult family home materially changes the ambiguous-loss visit. The 1:3 caregiver-to-resident ratio, the consistency of the same small staff team week over week, and the residential scale of the environment mean the resident's nervous system arrives at the visit less depleted by ambient stimulation than it would in a 60-unit memory care wing. Behavioral symptoms during family visits are consistently lower in the small home model, which in turn sustains family visit frequency. This is the structural reason Washington State's long-standing investment in adult family homes (roughly 3,000 statewide, 21 in Burien alone, the highest per-capita concentration in the country) is a clinically meaningful option for moderate to advanced dementia placement, particularly when family visit sustainability is a primary care-plan goal.

At Burien Best Care Home we accept COPES placements and we work with referring colleagues on pending applications case by case. Happy to be a clinical and referral resource for King County physicians and care managers working with dementia families in the ambiguous-loss stage.
