## 1. FACEBOOK POST (Primary, adult children + seniors)

**Suggested image concept:** Overhead shot of a real kitchen counter at 1am. A laptop open with a search bar glowing. A bowl of cereal, half-eaten, spoon resting inside. A mug of tea gone cold. **Post copy:**

There is a moment that happens in almost every family. The daughter is awake at 1am at the kitchen counter. Her laptop is open. Her mother is asleep upstairs in the guest room, where she has been for three months now, ever since the second fall. The daughter types the same six words into the search bar, deletes them, types them again, deletes them, types them a third time and hits return.

*How do I know when it is time.*

I have sat across the kitchen table from a hundred versions of that daughter here in Burien. I was also, twenty years ago, her.

Here is what I want her to know, honestly, because nobody told me.

Most families do not recognize the right time when they are in it. They recognize it about six months later, looking back from a hospital bed. That is not a flaw in the family. That is the structure of the decision. The signs almost never arrive as one dramatic moment. They arrive as a pattern that has been going on for a year, and each individual sign gets explained away because it feels ordinary. A fall is a fall. A confused phone call is a confused phone call.

If you are counting the signs right now, you are not jumping the gun. You are catching up to a reality that has already arrived.

The useful distinction is not "is it time yet." The useful distinction is crisis versus warning. A crisis is a 911 call, an ambulance, a discharge planner saying she cannot go home. You get forty-eight hours to make a decision that should have taken six weeks. A warning is everything that comes before that. The smaller fall she "sat down" from. The pill organizer that is not getting filled right. The mail piling up. The Saturday you noticed she was wearing the same shirt you saw her in last Saturday.

Families who act during the warning phase have time. Time to tour, time to plan finances, time to involve your parent in whatever way your parent can still participate. Families who act during the crisis phase get whatever bed is open, often at the highest price, often in the worst week of their lives.

You are not waiting for evidence. You are waiting for permission.

You do not need permission. You need one cup of coffee at a real kitchen table in a real home in Burien, with someone who has been through it, with no clipboard and no pressure. Just so you can see.

The full piece is on the blog today. If you want to drive over and sit at a kitchen table in Burien, we are here.

## 4. LINKEDIN POST (healthcare professionals + discharge planners + referral network)

**Suggested image concept:** A quiet residential kitchen in soft daylight. A cup of coffee on a table, a laptop closed, reading glasses on top of a notebook, a sage napkin. No people. The visual equivalent of the morning after a long search. Implies: a family has been sitting here thinking.

**Post copy:**

A note for my colleagues in discharge planning, social work, geriatric care management, and primary care across King County.

The most common question we hear from families touring our adult family home in Burien is not "what does it cost" or "what services do you offer." It is "how do I know when it's time?"

By the time families are asking that question out loud, most of them have been watching a downward trajectory at home for twelve to eighteen months. They have explained away each individual sign because the sign on its own felt ordinary. A fall. A medication error. A confused phone call. The pattern only becomes visible in aggregate, and by the time the pattern is fully visible, the family is either in a warning window or already in a crisis.

From the operator side, and from twenty years on the frontline of this decision, I can tell you the structural reality. National data continues to hold steady. Approximately one in four families who reach out for senior care need it immediately, and another one in four need it within thirty days. Roughly 95 percent of the families we meet arrive in some version of crisis. That is not a planning timeline. That is a reactive timeline, and it costs families more money, more dignity, and more peace of mind than the same decision would have cost them six months earlier.

The clinical reframe that seems to help families most is the distinction between crisis and warning. A crisis is the 911 call, the ER admit, the discharge planner's phone call to us on a Friday afternoon for a Sunday placement. A warning is everything that comes before. Families who act in the warning window have time to tour, time to plan finances, time to involve the parent in the decision to the extent the parent can still participate. Families who act in the crisis window take whatever is available.

Two practical asks for those of us in the referral pathway.

First, when you are seeing warning signs in a primary care visit, a home health visit, or a hospital discharge, please name the phase to the family. "This is a warning. You are still in a window. You have time. Use it." Families will not name the window themselves. They will pattern-match to ordinary aging and wait.

Second, if you have a family in the warning window who would benefit from a no-commitment tour of a six-bed adult family home in Burien, White Center, Normandy Park, or South King County, send them our way. They do not have to decide anything. They just have to see.
