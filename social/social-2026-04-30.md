It is Thursday in Burien, ten days out from Mother's Day. By Sunday, May 10, you will know whether your mother is having a quiet day or a long one. You will know whether the morning got off to a hard start or a soft one. You do not know that yet. None of us does. So this week, while the question of what Mother's Day will look like is still abstract, I want to walk you through what eleven o'clock on Sunday morning actually looks like inside our six-bed adult family home in Burien, so the day stops being abstract and starts being a place you can already picture her sitting in.

At eleven o'clock on Sunday, your mother will be in the chair by the west-facing window. The lap blanket she likes, the soft sage one with the slightly worn binding on the corner she rubs with her thumb when she is thinking about something, will be folded over the arm of the chair. The Roberta Flack record her husband bought her in 1973 will be on the turntable, on the second pass through the A side, because the morning caregiver knows that *Killing Me Softly* settles her even on the days when nothing else does. There will be a halved buttermilk pancake and a small pile of scrambled eggs on a blue-and-white plate on the tray we keep for her, because eating from the wooden tray on her lap goes down easier than eating at the dining table on her hard mornings, and we follow her cues on which kind of morning it is. The caregiver will be in the kitchen, folding a load of laundry that came out of the dryer at 10:30. Present. Not hovering. Within earshot.

That is the eleven o'clock you are walking into.

If you arrive at eleven, the visit does not have to be performed. You do not have to drive across town in Mother's Day brunch traffic. You do not have to make a reservation for a meal she cannot really eat anymore. You do not have to compete with thirty other families in a banquet room with carpet she does not recognize. You walk in the front door, the same door you have walked in dozens of times now, and the caregiver looks up from the laundry and says, by name, "Mary, your daughter Sarah is here." You sit in the second chair, the one we moved next to hers in February so the side table is between you instead of in front of you. You take her hand. You sit in the same room, in the same light, with the same record playing, that she has been sitting in all morning. You do not change anything about the morning. You just join it.

This is the part nobody tells you when you are first researching adult family homes versus assisted living versus memory care. The size of the home is not only a staffing-ratio argument. The size of the home is the holiday execution argument. In our six-bed home, Mother's Day is not an event that the building has to put on for fifty residents at once. There is no schedule to keep. There is no nine-to-eleven brunch shift and an eleven-to-one brunch shift and a sing-along at two. There is just Sunday in the same living room, with the same caregiver who already knows what she eats and what record she likes and which lap blanket she rubs the corner of when she is thinking. The day belongs to her. Then it belongs to you and her together. Then it belongs to her again after you leave.

You are allowed to come at eleven. You are allowed to come at three. You are allowed to come twice in the same day, for twenty minutes each time, if her stamina is short and your nervous system is shorter. You are allowed to bring tulips from QFC and put them in the small terracotta vase on the side table next to the ones I will already have put there on Saturday. You are allowed to bring a folded copy of an old church bulletin she used to read, or a soft cardigan she has not worn in years, or a small jar of the lemon hand cream she always had on her bathroom counter in the house on the hill before the house got too big. You are allowed to bring the grandkids for fifteen minutes and let the caregiver and I take them to the back porch with juice boxes while you have her to yourself for a while. You are allowed to bring nothing at all and sit in the second chair and not say anything and let the second pass through the A side of the record finish.

What you do not have to do, and what families coming out of the first hard year of dementia care need permission to stop doing, is perform Mother's Day. The version of your mother who hosted the brunch, who set the table with the good linens, who stood at the stove pouring coffee for everyone and remembering who took it black and who took it with cream, that version of her was the host. You spent forty years being her guest. The hardest part of this Sunday for a lot of daughters in Burien this year is going to be the moment the role flips, and the daughter realizes that on this particular Mother's Day she is the host and her mother is the guest. That moment is grief. Name it grief. You are not failing the day. You are grieving the host.

Then walk in the front door at eleven, take her hand, sit down in the second chair, and let the record play. That is the day. It does not have to be more than the day.

If you are anywhere in Burien, White Center, Normandy Park, West Seattle, or anywhere in South King County, and you are looking at next Sunday with the kind of dread that has no name yet, I want you to know there is a chair by a window already waiting. There is a record already on the turntable. There is a caregiver already learning your mother's morning. We have been doing this in Burien for twenty years. Mother's Day is not an event we put on. It is a Sunday we already know how to hold.

Reach out anytime. The chair is the same. The window is the same. The hands holding hers are the same. That is the gift the small home gives you. The day that does not have to be performed.

A note for the King County referral network ten days out from Mother's Day, Sunday, May 10, 2026.

The first holiday after a parent moves into residential dementia care is among the most clinically fragile windows we hold. Families arrive at the door carrying anticipatory grief, role-reversal shame, and the particular exhaustion of a host who has lost her host. We know from the literature on caregiver burden, validation therapy, and ambiguous loss what is happening underneath. We do not always have language to give the family on the way in.

In our six-bed adult family home in Burien, Sunday morning, May 10, will not be an event. It will be a Sunday. The same chair by the west-facing window. The same sage lap blanket. The same record on the turntable. The same caregiver folding laundry while a daughter takes her mother's hand and lets the record finish. The structural advantage of a small home on holidays is not a marketing line. It is a clinical observation. Continuity of caregivers, continuity of room, continuity of pacing, and the absence of a fifty-resident brunch shift is, in itself, a form of dementia-informed holiday care.

If you are working with a King County family in the first holiday window after placement and the family needs permission to arrive at eleven instead of nine, to bring nothing, to leave after twenty minutes, or to skip the day entirely and try again at Father's Day, we have a one-page handout we are happy to share for your intake folders or family education packets. Reach out anytime.
