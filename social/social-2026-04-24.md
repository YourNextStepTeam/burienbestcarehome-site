**Post copy:**

It is a Tuesday afternoon in Burien. The daughter is standing in her mother's kitchen, looking at a plate that took her forty minutes to prepare. Half a scrambled egg. A quarter piece of toast. Three slices of strawberry, arranged in the small fan her mother used to arrange for her when she was eight years old.

Her mother pushes the plate away with the same gentle, distracted motion a toddler uses to push away a blanket.

"I'm not hungry, honey."

Later, in her car in the driveway, the daughter will search on her phone. "Dementia mom won't eat." She will scroll past twenty articles about small portions and finger foods and Ensure shakes. She will read that forty percent of people with dementia experience significant weight loss. She will feel a hot ring of panic in her chest. She will text her sister a picture of the plate.

If you have been the daughter in that driveway this week, I want to tell you what twenty years in senior care in Burien has taught me about that plate.

Food was never the love. Presence was the love. Food was just the delivery vehicle your mother used because she had a body and a kitchen and you had a mouth and a stomach. Now she has less body, less appetite, and less fluency with the kitchen. The delivery vehicle has to change. That is not a demotion. That is a translation.

The first thing I want you to hear is that if your mother has lost five pounds in a week or ten pounds in a month, you are not watching a slow drift. You are past the threshold and you need a doctor visit this week, not next month. About one third of sudden eating refusals in older adults has a reversible cause. A UTI in an elder does not look like a UTI, it looks like confusion and refusal. Constipation, a mouth ulcer, a loose denture, undiagnosed pain, a new medication, or a medication dose that needs adjusting can all shut appetite down in days. You are not panicking. You are triaging.

The second thing I want you to hear is that if the workup comes back clean and the pattern keeps deepening, your mother is not trying to hurt you by refusing food. Dementia changes the brain structures that regulate hunger, satiety, taste, and smell. She may look at a plate and see shapes. She may chew and forget to swallow. She may feel full after three bites. Up to fifty seven percent of people with dementia experience eating or swallowing disturbances, and none of those people made that choice on purpose.

The third thing I want you to hear is the one most articles will not say directly. In late stage dementia, the body begins a gradual, orderly withdrawal from the work of staying alive. Appetite is usually the first thing to quiet down. This is not a symptom to be fought. It is the body's oldest language for saying, I am beginning to let go. A hospice consult at this moment is not giving up. In Washington, hospice is a Medicare benefit that adds a nurse, a home health aide, a social worker, and a twenty four hour on-call line. The research is clear that dementia patients who enroll in hospice often live as long or longer than those who do not, because they stop getting hospital transfers that cause setbacks and they live in calmer environments. You can call Community Living Connections at 1-855-567-0252 for a free walk-through of what hospice looks like in King County before you decide anything.

Here is what almost no article about dementia nutrition will tell you, and it is the thing I most want you to hear if you are reading this in your car.

You have not failed her. The plate that keeps coming back full is not a report card on your love. It is a message from a body and a brain that are doing what bodies and brains eventually do.

The most loving thing you can do at this kitchen table right now is put down the fork, take her hand, look out the window at the bird feeder she has stopped filling, and tell her about the bird you saw on your way over. Do not ask if she is hungry. Just sit.

If the weight of being the daughter and the case manager and the nurse and the food police is finally too much, and you are starting to wonder whether your mother might eat more in a small home with trained caregivers who know her by name and can serve five small plates across ninety minutes instead of three meals on a schedule, I am here. ### Slide 2 (Sage background, cream text, typographic):

**Dementia refusal is not a behavior problem. It is one of four things.**

**One.** A reversible medical cause. A UTI in an elder does not look like a UTI, it looks like confusion and refusal. Constipation, a mouth ulcer, a loose denture, undiagnosed pain, a new medication dose. About one third of sudden eating refusals in older adults have a cause a doctor can fix in a week.

**Two.** Dementia itself. The brain structures that regulate hunger, taste, and swallowing are no longer intact. She is not being stubborn. She is being neurological.

**Three.** Depression, grief, or a loud environment. Older adults who eat alone eat less. Overhead lights, a TV, and a hovering fork all reduce intake.

**Four.** The body's natural withdrawal at the end of a dementia journey. This one is not a problem to be solved. It is a message to be honored.

Your job this week is not to decide which of the four it is. Your job is to rule out number one with a doctor visit, reduce the friction of two and three, and ask the hospice question if the pattern starts looking like four.

### Slide 4 (Sage background, cream text, typographic reference slide built to be screenshot and forwarded to a sibling):

**Five small shifts that work better than one big meal.**

**Calorie density beats volume every time.** A half cup of whole milk Greek yogurt with a tablespoon of almond butter and a drizzle of honey delivers more useful nutrition than a full plate she will refuse. This is not the moment for low fat anything.

**Serving size matters more than you think.** A full plate overwhelms a dementia brain. Try one food at a time on a small plate. One piece of toast in four triangles. Two minutes later, three strawberries. Ten minutes later, a teacup of tomato soup. Five small offerings across ninety minutes almost always outperform one meal.

**Color contrast helps.** A white plate on a white placemat with pale food disappears for a dementia brain. Mashed potatoes on a blue plate. Applesauce in a red bowl. Research shows it increases intake by up to twenty five percent in some studies.

**Timing follows the person, not the clock.** Most people with dementia eat better earlier in the day. Bigger portion before ten a.m. A grazing pattern across the day beats three scheduled meals. This is why institutional mealtimes often fail dementia residents and why smaller homes usually do better.

**Hydration matters more than food in the short term.** Dehydration drops appetite fast. Popsicles, watermelon, broth, and thin smoothies all count. A person who hates drinking water will often eat an ice pop.

**Post copy (240 words):**

A note for colleagues in hospice, memory care, and geriatric primary care, for the next family who arrives at your door with a plate that keeps coming back full.

Dementia weight loss is almost always framed in family caregiver content as a nutritional problem to be solved with Ensure shakes, finger foods, and bright plates. That framing collapses three very different clinical moments into one, and it is the framing that is keeping families out of the hospice conversation for months longer than they should be.

Three clinical reframes I offer families who arrive in my kitchen in Burien:

First, the reversible-cause workup. One third of sudden eating refusals in older adults have a cause a geriatrician can address in a week. A urinalysis, a medication review, a dental check, a thyroid panel, and a basic metabolic panel are the non-negotiable first move before any food-refusal narrative hardens into "it is the dementia."

Second, the neurological reframe. Up to 57 percent of dementia patients experience eating or swallowing disturbances that are a function of changing brain structure, not choice. The family needs to hear this from a clinician, not from a blog.

Third, the end-of-life reframe. In late-stage dementia, appetite withdrawal is the body's natural, painless beginning of letting go. Hospice enrollment at that point extends quality of life and often extends length of life in the dementia population, per current hospice research. Families wait far too long.

If helpful in your next family conversation, please use freely.

Washington families can reach Community Living Connections at 1-855-567-0252 for a free hospice eligibility walkthrough. Alzheimer's Association Western and Central Washington 24 hour helpline 1-800-272-3900.
