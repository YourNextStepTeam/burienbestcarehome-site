## 1. FACEBOOK POST (Primary, adult children + seniors)

**Suggested image concept:** A kitchen table at Burien Best Care Home, mid-morning. Half-full coffee mug, a folded newspaper, a half-finished crossword, reading glasses resting on top. Warm natural light through a window. Sage green napkin. No people in frame. The feeling of an interrupted, ordinary morning. Soft, candid, not staged.

**Post copy:**

If you are touring adult family homes this weekend in Burien or anywhere in King County, there is one thing I wish someone had told me before my first tour, back when I was doing this for my own family.

You are not looking for a perfect home. There is no such thing. You are looking for a home that is actually being lived in, by actual people, on the actual day you are standing in it.

I have been on both sides of that kitchen table. I have walked through three homes in one Saturday, trying to remember which one had the sunroom. I have also, on the other side of it, watched families do a forty-five minute walkthrough and then try to make a six-figure, multi-year, life-shaping decision from a folder of laminated pages.

The checklist is not wrong. It is just not enough.

Here is the truth. What matters most on a tour is not in any checklist. Stand in the entryway for ninety seconds and notice. Can you hear a television, a dish being rinsed, a conversation, a dog? Is there a half-finished crossword on the coffee table? Is there a resident in the living room awake, being a person?

Or has the house been cleared and quieted for your arrival? You do not want a staged house. You want a lived-in one.

A few honest signals to read. Smell (faintly of this morning's breakfast, not a wall of Febreze). Sound (the house doing something, not silence). Sight (residents dressed, hair brushed, glasses on, sitting comfortably). And the greeter (does she know the residents' names, does she touch them gently in passing, do they brighten when she walks in).

If something feels off and you cannot name it, trust the feeling. If something feels like exhaling for the first time in six months, come back for a second visit and see if it holds.

## 4. LINKEDIN POST (healthcare professionals + discharge planners + referral network)

**Suggested image concept:** A warm, candid shot of a residential entryway or kitchen in soft natural light. Coat on a hook, reading glasses on a side table, a folded newspaper. No people. The visual equivalent of an interrupted morning. Implies lived-in, not facility.

**Post copy:**

A note for my colleagues in discharge planning, social work, geriatric care management, and primary care across King County.

When a family tours adult family homes, most of them arrive with a printed checklist of thirty or so items. Most of those checklists are adapted from large-facility assessment tools. Applied to a six bed residential home, they produce a false sense of thoroughness and a real inability to differentiate between homes.

From the operator side, the items that matter most on a tour are almost never on the checklist.

First, whether the home is actually being lived in on the day of the tour, or whether it has been cleared, quieted, and staged. A lived-in home reveals itself in the first ninety seconds through ambient sound, kitchen activity, resident presence in common areas, and the greeter's behavior toward residents in passing. A staged home reveals itself through silence, heavy air freshener, and residents parked together in front of a single TV.

Second, whether the caregiver knows each resident's name, history, and preferences. In a six bed home with 1 to 3 staffing, this is observable in thirty seconds. A greeter who walks past residents as though they are furniture is telling you something the checklist cannot.

Third, whether the operator will let the family see the actual room, the actual shared bathroom, and the actual caregiver on shift that day. If the tour is restricted to a showcase room and a binder, that is itself a data point.

Two practical implications for those of us in the referral pathway.

One. When coaching families on tours, reframe the goal. They are not running a compliance audit. They are reading a house. Encourage them to notice three things only. How the residents look. How the caregiver treats the residents. Whether they can picture their parent in that living room at 3pm on a Wednesday.

Two. Trust the family's instinct. Adult children who have been monitoring a parent for a year or two have developed a sharper safety instinct than any published rubric. If a family reports a gut no about a facility that scored well on paper, take it seriously.

At Burien Best Care Home, we do not clear the house for tours. Families are welcome to walk into a real morning, meet whoever is working that day, and ask the hard questions. Happy to coordinate on any Burien, White Center, Normandy Park, or South King County family currently in the tour phase.
