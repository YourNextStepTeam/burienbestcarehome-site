**Post copy:**

It is a Tuesday evening in Burien. Twelve days until Mother's Day.

The daughter is at her own kitchen counter, slicing an apple for her 9-year-old's lunchbox for tomorrow. The dishwasher is running. The 13-year-old is on the couch in the next room with headphones on. Soccer practice is in twenty minutes.

Her 9-year-old is at the kitchen table doing a math worksheet. She looks up, in the careful flat voice nine-year-olds use when they have been thinking about a question for a while, and asks her mother whether Grandma is scary now.

The daughter stops slicing.

The 13-year-old, who is supposedly listening to music, takes off one headphone.

The question has been sitting in this house for three weeks. The daughter has been carrying it on the inside of her chest like a held breath. Mother's Day is twelve days away. Her mother lives in an adult family home down the road. The 13-year-old has not visited since Thanksgiving. The 9-year-old visited once in February, did not say a word the whole time, and asked on the drive home why Grandma kept calling her by Mom's name.

The daughter does not know yet whether she is bringing the kids on May 10.

If you are that mother, somebody's daughter and somebody's mother at once, standing at your own kitchen counter trying to answer a question that you have not yet answered for yourself, here is what twenty years in Burien senior care has taught me.

The question is not whether to bring the grandkids.

The question is what relationship you want them to have with their grandmother in this season of her life, and whether you trust them to handle the truth of who she is now.

Children do not arrive at a dementia visit holding the script you arrive holding. They do not have thirty years of "Grandma should be" running in the background. Your 9-year-old does not remember the version of your mother who took her to the cherry-blossom drive in 2019. Your 9-year-old has the version of your mother who calls her by your name and pats her hand. That is not a failure of your daughter's experience. That is your daughter's actual grandmother. The one she has now.

Children, in my experience over the years, are often the easiest visitors a person with dementia gets. They are short. They are warm. They do not test her with questions. They are patient with repetition because their world is built on repetition. They climb into a lap. They show a drawing. They say, "I made you this." They do not need to be remembered to be received.

Your 13-year-old is a different question. Older grandchildren are aware of the loss in a way younger children are not. They remember the grandmother from before. They are also figuring out, at 13, who they are, and they do not always have room for somebody else's grief on top of their own. You are allowed to give your 13-year-old the same permission you have not yet given yourself. The permission to grieve at her own pace, not the calendar's, not Mother's Day's, not yours.

You are allowed to bring only the 9-year-old.

You are allowed to bring them both for twenty minutes and leave.

You are allowed to let your 13-year-old visit through a phone screen for now, on FaceTime, with her grandmother propped up in the recliner she likes.

You are allowed to not bring them at all this year and try again at Father's Day.

If you do bring them, please bring them small. Twenty minutes is plenty. Bring one familiar activity that does not depend on memory. A photo album you turn the pages of together. A song she used to sing to them when they were toddlers. A simple craft, like coloring the same coloring page side by side. A snack you all eat at the kitchen table. Stay close enough that your kids can climb into your lap if they need to. Never leave a child alone with a grandparent with dementia, not because anything bad will happen, but because your child needs an exit door, and you are her exit door.

If your mother is in a small adult family home, this part actually works. In a 6-bed home, your kids walk into a real living room with a real couch and a real kitchen, and there is often soup on the stove and a dog asleep in a sun patch and a back porch they can take a snack to when it gets too much. There are caregivers who already remember your kids' names from the last visit and ask the 9-year-old how soccer is going. Children can move in a small home in a way they cannot move in a long fluorescent corridor.

Then debrief in the car on the way home, before your kids walk back into school the next day with that visit still open inside them. Ask your 9-year-old what surprised her. Ask your 13-year-old, if she came, what was the hardest part. Tell them the part of your visit that was hard for you. Tell them what was good. Tell them you are proud of them. If they cry, sit with them. If they laugh at something Grandma said, laugh with them. The disease is not the only thing in the room. There is also still her, and there is also still them, and there is also still you, the bridge between the two.

Here is the thing nobody told me when I was first walking my own family through this. You are not deciding whether your children get to have a grandmother. You are deciding what kind of relationship she gets to have with them in this season. Both versions of her count. The one who knew their names. The one who does not. They are still her grandchildren. She is still their grandmother. That does not require her memory to be true.

If you are still in the in-between, still standing at your own kitchen counter twelve days before Mother's Day, you can find me at burienbestcarehome.com. I am happy to sit on the phone with you, or at our kitchen table here in Burien, and walk through what a twenty-minute visit could look like with your 9-year-old, or what a FaceTime drop-in could look like with your 13-year-old, or what your Mother's Day Sunday could look like if the kids stay home this year and you come alone.

You are not failing at being a daughter when you protect your kids. You are not failing at being a mother when you visit your own. You are doing both jobs in the same week, in the same hour, in the same kitchen. That is not a contradiction. That is what this season asks of you.

Bring them small. Bring one of them. Bring none of them. Bring them on a phone screen. The shape of love is not measured in the number of bodies in the room.

## 4. LINKEDIN POST (healthcare professionals + referral network)

The multigenerational dementia visit is one of the most under-supported moments in family caregiving practice.

We talk about the spousal caregiver and the adult-child caregiver. We rarely talk about the sandwich-generation caregiver who is also a parent of school-age children, choosing on a Tuesday evening whether to bring her own kids to a holiday visit with her mother in residential dementia care.

Twelve days before Mother's Day, that decision is being made in kitchens across King County right now.

The clinical and family-systems literature is clear on the basic guidance. Prepare children with simple, honest, age-appropriate language. Keep visits short, twenty to thirty minutes. Bring a familiar non-memory-dependent activity (music, photo albums, simple crafts). Never leave a child alone with a grandparent with dementia. Debrief on the way home before the child re-enters their school day. The AARP, the Alzheimer's Association, Alzheimers.gov, and the Family Caregiver Alliance all converge on this short list.

What the published guidance underweights is the asymmetry between younger and older grandchildren, and the absence of permission language for partial participation.

In practice, younger children (under 10) are often the most resilient and the most receivable visitors. They have less script for who Grandma "should be." They show up with a drawing. They climb into a lap. They are warm and short and patient with repetition because their own world is built on repetition.

Older grandchildren (11 to 17) face a different and harder set of feelings. They remember the grandmother from before. They are also doing developmental identity work that does not always have room for somebody else's grief on top of their own. Permission to opt out of the in-person visit, to participate via FaceTime, or to wait until Father's Day, is clinically appropriate and is rarely offered to families.

For colleagues across the King County referral network, when a sandwich-generation primary caregiver mentions an upcoming Mother's Day visit and has children, please consider asking the explicit question: "What is your plan for your kids' participation, and would it be helpful to talk through partial-participation options like FaceTime, abbreviated in-person visits, or splitting which children attend?" That single question reduces the silent weight a parent is carrying for two generations at once.

In small adult family home settings, including 6-bed homes, the multigenerational visit is structurally easier than it is in larger facilities. Real living rooms, real kitchens, backyards, and continuity of caregivers who learn the grandchildren's names mean children can actually move and re-regulate during a visit, instead of sitting still in a fluorescent corridor.

Mother's Day on May 10 is twelve days out. If you serve dementia families in King County and want a one-page caregiver handout I am drafting on the multigenerational dementia visit (pre-visit prep language for kids by age, partial-participation options, the in-car debrief), please message me. Happy to share with hospital social workers, hospice intake, geriatric care managers, school counselors and pediatric mental-health colleagues across Providence Hospice of Seattle, EvergreenHealth, UW Memory and Brain Wellness Center, Virginia Mason Franciscan, and Seattle Children's social work.
